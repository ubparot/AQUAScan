2  IDENTIFICATION AND DEFINITION OF THE PROBLEM  Water quality monitoring is an important part of protecting aquatic ecosystems because it helps scientists, restoration groups, and environmental agencies understand the condition of a body of water and identify changes over time. Parameters such as dissolved oxygen, turbidity, temperature, pH, salinity, and dissolved solids can reveal whether an aquatic environment is healthy enough to support aquatic life and maintain ecosystem balance. However, pollution, runoff, nutrient loading, and changing environmental conditions have placed increasing pressure on many water bodies, creating a growing need for more accurate and useful monitoring. This issue is especially relevant in regions such as the Chesapeake Bay, where water quality remains an important part of ecosystem recovery and long-term environmental management.  Although there are already many ways to collect water-quality data, current methods still struggle to capture water conditions completely and consistently. This is especially true in aquatic environments where conditions can change over short distances, over short periods of time, and vertically through the water column. In many cases, the data being collected is too limited, too delayed, or too generalized to fully represent what is happening in the water at a given time. As a result, researchers and environmental organizations are often left with an incomplete understanding of local water conditions. Current monitoring gaps can be seen in several areas:  1.   Lack of Depth-Resolved Data:   Depth-based water-quality monitoring is still very limited, even though conditions can vary significantly through the water column. Dissolved oxygen, temperature, turbidity, and salinity can all change vertically, and without measurements at multiple depths, low-oxygen bottom waters, stratification, and other below-surface conditions can go undetected. This leaves an incomplete picture of actual ecosystem health and can hide serious environmental stress that is not visible from surface data alone.  2.   Low Spatial and Temporal Resolution:   Many traditional monitoring methods collect data at limited locations or at wide time intervals. This can miss short-term changes, localized pollution events, and smaller problem areas that may still have a major effect on surrounding ecosystems. When data is too spread out in time or location, it becomes harder to recognize patterns and respond effectively.  3.   Dependence on Manual and Laboratory-Based Sampling:   A large amount of water-quality monitoring still depends on collecting samples manually and transporting them for later analysis. This can delay results, reduce sampling frequency, require trained personnel, and make consistent monitoring more difficult and more expensive over time. It also makes it harder to gather immediate feedback in the field.  4.   Limited Accessibility in Nearshore and Sheltered Environments:   Larger survey boats and broad-scale monitoring methods are not always suited for smaller, more complex aquatic areas. Shoreline zones, sheltered regions, and other difficult-to-access environments can be harder to monitor consistently, even though these areas are often environmentally important and can experience significant changes in water quality.  In summary, the problem in aquatic water-quality monitoring comes from the lack of detailed, real-time, and depth-aware data in environments where strong monitoring is often needed most.  These limitations create challenges not only for scientific understanding, but also for ecosystem restoration and environmental management. If the available data is incomplete, then the decisions made from that data will also be limited. The resolution of this problem will require innovative engineering solutions that improve how water-quality conditions are monitored across different locations and depths while making data collection more effective, more immediate, and more practical in complex aquatic environments.

3  INFORMATION GATHERING: ENGINEERING THE TOOLS OF SCIENTIFIC DISCOVERY  Scientific discovery depends on tools that allow researchers to observe, measure, and understand the world more accurately. From instruments and sensors to digital monitoring systems and mapping software, engineering has continually expanded what scientists are able to study and explain. In environmental science, these tools are especially important because they help researchers track changing conditions, detect patterns, and collect information that cannot be judged by appearance alone. As environmental challenges become more complex, the need for stronger and more efficient tools of scientific discovery continues to increase.  Why a Solution is Needed  1.   Environmental problems require stronger data collection:   Pollution, runoff, nutrient loading, habitat loss, and other environmental pressures cannot be understood or addressed effectively without accurate information. Researchers and environmental organizations need tools that can collect meaningful data, reveal patterns, and show where problems are developing and how severe they are.  2.   Current monitoring can be slow, expensive, and difficult to scale:   Traditional field sampling and later laboratory analysis often require repeated site visits, trained personnel, specialized equipment, and additional time before results can be interpreted. Even many fixed monitoring systems only represent one location at a time, which limits how much area they can actually describe. This creates a need for tools that are more efficient, more flexible, and more practical for collecting data in real-world conditions.  3.   Scientific data must be presented in ways people can actually use:   Measurements alone do not automatically lead to understanding. Data needs to be organized, processed, and displayed clearly through graphs, models, maps, and other visual formats so that patterns can be recognized, changes can be compared, and results can support stronger scientific and environmental decisions.  How the Challenge Evolved (Historical Perspective)  The tools of scientific discovery have developed over a very long period of time. In the ancient world, early astronomers used simple measuring tools to track the movement of the sun, stars, and seasons, showing that scientific progress has always depended on better ways to observe and record information. In 1807, the United States Coast Survey began systematic coastal surveys, demonstrating how organized measurement could improve scientific understanding of real environments. In 1934, Arnold Beckman invented the first commercially successful electronic pH meter, giving researchers a faster and more reliable way to measure chemical conditions. Since then, digital probes, real-time monitoring systems, remote sensing, and computer-based analysis have continued to improve the speed, detail, and usefulness of scientific data. Even with these advancements, many challenges still require tools that are more detailed, more adaptable, and better suited for collecting information in complex real-world environments.  Why It Matters to Us All  Improving the tools of scientific discovery matters because stronger tools lead to stronger data, and stronger data supports better decisions. This affects people and ecosystems in several important ways:  ●   Healthier Ecosystems:   Better monitoring and research tools make it easier to identify environmental stress, guide restoration efforts, and support long-term ecosystem protection. When scientists and agencies can detect problems earlier and understand them more clearly, they are better able to protect habitats and wildlife.  ●   Greater Economic Stability:   Better environmental data can help protect industries and resources that depend on healthy natural systems. This supports long-term economic stability by helping reduce environmental damage that could negatively affect local economies over time.  ●   Stronger Communities:   Many communities rely on natural systems for fisheries, recreation, tourism, and environmental resources. Better scientific tools help protect those systems by improving the quality of the decisions made about them and supporting a healthier environment for the people who depend on it.

4  POSSIBLE SOLUTION #1: Buoy Monitoring Network with Multi-Depth Sensors  Traditional water-quality monitoring often struggles to show how conditions change throughout a body of water. Fixed surface stations can provide useful information, but they usually represent one point and may not show what is happening below the surface. One possible solution is buoy monitoring network with multi-depth sensors. This approach would use anchored buoy stations placed throughout a study area, with water-quality sensors positioned at multiple depths to continuously monitor changing conditions. By combining continuous collection with depth-based measurements, this system would provide more complete and immediate data for aquatic research.  Technical Rationale  ●   Multi-Depth Sensor Strings:   This solution would use vertically distributed sensors or sensor strings to measure water-quality parameters at multiple depths rather than only at the surface. This allows the system to directly capture water-column variation instead of relying on one surface reading to represent the entire area.  ●   Anchored Buoy Platforms:   Fixed buoy platforms would keep the monitoring system in stable locations over long periods of time. This makes repeated measurements from the same points possible and supports consistent environmental observation.  ●   Telemetry and Continuous Data Logging:   Buoy systems can use onboard data loggers and telemetry systems to record and transmit measurements over time. This allows the stations to collect information continuously instead of only during occasional sampling events.  Engineering Feasibility  ●   Existing Technology Base:   Water-quality sondes, buoy platforms, telemetry systems, and waterproof sensor housings already exist and are used in environmental monitoring. This makes the general concept technically realistic.  ●   Practical System Design:   Sensors for dissolved oxygen, pH, turbidity, conductivity, salinity, and temperature can all be integrated into a fixed buoy system. Multiple sensors or a profiling setup could be used to monitor different depths.  ●   High Deployment and Maintenance Demand:   Although the system is realistic, building a full monitoring network would require anchoring hardware, power systems, installation effort, maintenance schedules, and reliable data transmission at every station. Covering a large region would make the system expensive to install and difficult to expand to new sites.  Comparison to Existing Technology  1.   Improved upon traditional fixed surface stations:   Many existing buoy and station systems already provide continuous monitoring, which shows that this type of approach is realistic. However, many of those systems mainly focus on surface or near-surface conditions, while this solution expands that idea by collecting data at multiple depths.  2.   More complete data than single-point monitoring:   A traditional fixed station only represents one location. A distributed buoy network would improve on that by collecting continuous data across multiple regions while also showing how conditions change through the water column.  3.   Builds on existing monitoring concepts:   Because buoy platforms and water-quality sensors are already used in monitoring programs, this solution does not require entirely new technology. Instead, it improves existing technology by combining networked coverage and multi-depth monitoring into one larger system.  Monitoring and Research Benefits  ●   Better Water-Column Understanding:   This system would help researchers identify stratification, low-oxygen bottom waters, and other below-surface conditions that surface readings may miss.  ●   Stronger Long-Term Monitoring:   Continuous buoy stations would create more consistent datasets, making it easier to study seasonal variation, recurring problems, and long-term environmental trends.  ●   Long-Term Environmental Pattern Detection:   Because the stations remain in fixed locations and collect data continuously, this system would be especially useful for identifying recurring environmental patterns in specific areas. That would help researchers observe how conditions change over time in the same locations rather than relying only on short-term surveys or isolated sampling events.

5  POSSIBLE SOLUTION #2: Using Drones to Enhance Water-Quality Monitoring  A major challenge in aquatic water-quality monitoring is that conditions can change quickly across a body of water while many current methods only collect data from limited locations. This makes it difficult to identify visible problem zones and environmental stress across a wider region. To address this issue, researchers have increasingly used remote sensing and aerial observation to monitor environmental conditions more efficiently. One possible solution is using drones to enhance water-quality monitoring. Drones can quickly survey large areas, reach sheltered or hard-to-access regions, and use imaging systems or targeted sampling attachments to investigate important locations. This makes drone-based monitoring a flexible approach for rapid environmental assessment and for identifying where more detailed follow-up monitoring is needed.  Technical Rationale  ●   GPS-Guided Flight Paths:   Drone systems can follow planned flight routes and return to selected locations in an organized and repeatable way. This allows researchers to survey a study area efficiently and revisit the same regions when needed.  ●   Thermal or Multispectral Imaging:   Drones can use high-resolution, thermal, or multispectral imaging to identify visible surface irregularities such as algae concentration, sediment plumes, shoreline runoff, discoloration, or temperature differences. These technologies make it possible to detect surface-level patterns that may indicate environmental stress.  ●   Targeted Water-Contact Sampling or Sensing:   Some UAV systems can use floating attachments, lowered sensor packages, or water-sampling devices to directly collect measurements such as pH, dissolved oxygen, conductivity, or turbidity. This gives the system a more direct monitoring capability than aerial imaging alone.  Engineering Feasibility  ●   Existing Drone Platforms:   Commercial drones already exist with GPS positioning, programmable flight paths, and payload capacity for environmental monitoring. This makes the overall concept technically realistic.  ●   Established Sensor and Sampling Attachments:   Research has already demonstrated drone-mounted water-quality monitoring systems, showing that direct spot measurements from UAVs are plausible when the drone is equipped with the right hardware.  ●   Operational Limits:   Although the system is realistic, it still depends heavily on battery life, payload capacity, repeated flights, and weather conditions. Wind, glare, rain, and poor visibility can reduce reliability, and adding water-contact sampling hardware increases technical complexity and cost.  Comparison to Existing Technology  1.   Improved upon traditional surface observation:   Shore-based observation is limited in range and detail. Drone monitoring improves on this by allowing researchers to view larger sections of water from above while also identifying locations that may need closer study.  2.   More flexible than larger-scale remote sensing:   Compared to broader aerial or satellite observation, drones can be deployed when needed and can capture more detailed imagery of smaller study areas.  3.   Combines aerial survey with targeted spot measurements:   Many remote sensing methods focus only on surface imagery. Drone-based monitoring can improve on this by combining aerial assessment with direct spot sampling or sensor-based measurements in selected locations  Monitoring and Research Benefits  ●   Rapid Surface Assessment:   This system would allow researchers to quickly evaluate large areas of water and identify visible surface-level problem zones.  ●   Better Spatial Awareness:   Drone surveys would help show where unusual conditions are happening across a study area, making it easier to locate hotspots and prioritize follow-up monitoring.  ●   Targeted Environmental Checkups:   By combining fast aerial coverage with the ability to perform direct spot measurements in selected areas, this approach would help researchers investigate visible stress areas more quickly and decide where more detailed monitoring is needed.

6  POSSIBLE SOLUTION #3: Small Unmanned Surface Vessel for Water-Quality Monitoring  Water-quality conditions can vary over short distances, especially in nearshore, sheltered, or shallow environments. This makes it difficult for fixed stations or larger survey systems to capture local changes efficiently. One possible solution is using a small unmanned surface vessel for water-quality monitoring. A compact boat equipped with water-quality sensors could travel across a study area and collect direct measurements from multiple nearby points, giving researchers a more detailed picture than a single stationary reading. This would make the approach especially useful for focused monitoring in smaller aquatic areas.  Technical Rationale  ●   GPS Waypoint Navigation or Remote Guidance:   A small unmanned surface vessel can follow programmed GPS waypoints or be remotely guided through a study area. This allows it to travel to multiple sampling locations in an organized and repeatable way.  ●   Onboard Sensor Integration:   Water-quality sensors mounted on the vessel can directly contact the water and measure conditions such as temperature, turbidity, pH, dissolved oxygen, salinity, or dissolved solids. This provides actual environmental data instead of only surface imagery.  ●   Compact Electric Propulsion:   A small hull with electric motors allows the vessel to move efficiently through calm water while remaining compact enough for narrow, sheltered, or shallow areas. This makes the platform practical for targeted monitoring in smaller aquatic regions.  Engineering Feasibility  ●   Established System Components:   Small electric hulls, remote-control systems, GPS modules, waterproof electronics, and onboard microcontrollers already exist and are used in marine and environmental applications. This makes the concept technically realistic.  ●   Practical Sensor and Data Integration:   Batteries, onboard controllers, water-quality sensors, and data logging systems can all be integrated into a compact floating platform, allowing the vessel to function as a mobile monitoring tool rather than a fixed station.  ●   Operational Limits:   This approach still depends on runtime, waterproofing, navigation reliability, and payload capacity. It is also better suited for detailed monitoring within a smaller area than for covering a very large body of water, and a standard surface-vessel layout would mainly measure conditions at or near the surface.  Comparison to Existing Technology  1.   More detailed coverage than fixed monitoring platforms:   Many current systems rely on one fixed location or widely spaced sampling points. A small unmanned surface vessel would improve on that by collecting direct measurements from multiple nearby locations within the same study area.  2.   Better suited for sheltered and shallow areas than larger survey vessels:   Existing survey boats can collect strong data, but they are often less practical in marinas, coves, shoreline zones, and other calmer areas. A smaller unmanned vessel would be easier to deploy in those environments.  3.   More targeted than standard surface-only monitoring vessels:   Many small surface vessels are used for general monitoring or wider-area survey work. This solution focuses on closer point spacing and more detailed monitoring within a smaller area.  Monitoring and Research Benefits  ●   Detailed Small-Area Coverage:   This system would allow researchers to collect direct water-quality data from many nearby locations within one study area, making it easier to detect smaller local changes that a single station might miss.  ●   Direct Water-Quality Measurement:   Because the vessel carries sensors directly on the water, it would provide stronger environmental data than methods that only observe surface appearance from a distance.  ●   Improved Monitoring in Nearshore and Sheltered Areas:   This approach would help researchers study shallow marinas, shoreline zones, sheltered coves, and other smaller aquatic areas more effectively, improving environmental understanding in places that may be overlooked by larger systems.

8  CHOSEN SOLUTION: AquaScan (Combined Solution)  Overview:  AquaScan is a solution using the strongest advantages of our above 3 solutions combined into one. This solution uses a small semi-autonomous surface vessel along with a deployable multi-sensor probe system designed to help researchers and environmental organizations monitor water quality more effectively. It combines direct in-water sensing, GPS-based positioning, controlled depth sampling, and digital data output to improve how aquatic conditions are measured and interpreted. By combining mobility with below-surface measurement, AquaScan can collect more detailed and useful environmental data than many traditional monitoring methods that rely only on fixed stations, surface readings, or manual sampling.  The system integrates a dual-pontoon vessel design that supports the propulsion system, electronics, battery, and sensor deployment mechanism in one stable floating platform. The vessel travels to selected sampling points, stops, and lowers its probe beneath the surface to collect readings at controlled depths. This allows AquaScan to gather information through the water column instead of only measuring conditions near the surface. The deployable probe carries the sensor package, while the vessel provides movement, power, positioning, and communication back to the operator interface.  AquaScan addresses the theme   “Engineering the Tools of Scientific Discovery”   by improving how aquatic data is collected and used in water-quality research. Many current monitoring methods can collect useful information, but they are often limited by surface-only readings, fixed sampling locations, delayed manual collection, or data that is difficult to compare across depth and location. AquaScan improves on these limitations by connecting direct sensor measurements with GPS position and probe depth, giving researchers a clearer way to study changes within a body of water. This makes the system more useful for identifying environmental stress, comparing sampling points, and supporting decisions in aquatic research and restoration.  In summary, AquaScan combats this problem by integrating direct sensing, controlled depth monitoring, GPS-based sampling, and digital visualization into one practical research platform. The system not only improves how water-quality data is collected, but also makes that information easier to interpret and apply. By using AquaScan, researchers can gather stronger scientific information, support better environmental decision-making, and improve the protection and understanding of aquatic ecosystems.

9  Structural Design and Material Selection  AquaScan was structurally designed to support a heavy scientific payload while remaining stable, waterproof, and compact enough for nearshore and sheltered environments. Because the vessel carries a large battery, onboard electronics, motors, and a deployable probe, the hull and support structure had to be designed around flotation, reinforcement, sealing, and balance rather than speed alone.  Hull Configuration  The vessel uses a dual-pontoon layout as its primary flotation system. This gave AquaScan a wider base, improved stability, and created open space between the hulls for the probe deployment mechanism.  ●   Dual pontoons:   increase stability and distribute buoyancy across both sides of the vessel  ●   Pontoon dimensions:   ~ 42” long × 7” tall × 6" wide  ●   Estimated loaded draft:   3.5” of pontoon height submerged under operating load  ●   Open center span:   allows the probe to deploy vertically without interfering with the propulsion system  ●   Survey-oriented geometry : prioritizes control, balance, and payload support  Structural Support and Materials  The central tray spans between the two pontoons and supports the battery and electronics, so this area required additional strength. All major parts were 3D printed in PETG, since PETG provided a strong balance of durability, water resistance, printability, and cost for a functional aquatic prototype. The pontoons also include internal ribbing, which increases hull stiffness, helps prevent flexing across the long printed sections, and provides additional support without adding excessive weight. To keep the structure rigid under load, the tray was supported with printed truss features and reinforced using carbon fiber rods. This was especially important because the battery alone weighs ~23 lb, making the center structure one of the most heavily loaded areas of the vessel.  Waterproofing Strategy  Because AquaScan operates directly in water, sealing was a major design requirement. Although PETG is relatively water-resistant, 3D printed parts can still allow leakage through seams and layer lines. To solve this, the printed components were joined using plastic-welding methods, with marine-grade JB Weld added at key seams and connection points for additional bonding and sealing. After assembly, the hull was coated with epoxy resin as a final waterproofing layer.  ●   O-ring seals: used in removable lids, wire pass-throughs, and probe interfaces  ●   JB Weld reinforcement: adds bonding and sealing at key hull connections  ●   Silicone sealant: used for more permanent seals around sensor openings  ●   Epoxy coating: improves sealing and reduces permeability through print seams

10  Electronics and Power System  AquaScan’s electronics are centered in the sealed electronics tray mounted in the middle of the boat, which serves as the main hub for onboard power and control. The system is powered by a 12-volt, 100-amp-hour lithium battery, which supplies energy to both the boat and the probe and gives AquaScan an expected runtime of about 30-40 minutes. This central battery feeds the main onboard electronics, while fuses help protect the system and keep power routed to the correct components. The Arduino Mega acts as the main brain of the vessel, managing the overall system, while a surface ESP32 serves as the communication link between the operator interface and the onboard control system by receiving commands and passing that information to the Mega.  Power from the battery is also sent directly to the electronic speed controllers (ESCs), which regulate output to the two propulsion motors. In the probe, a second ESP32 acts as the local controller for the sensor package, reading the environmental sensors and sending that information back to the boat through the marine cable tether, which carries both power and data between the vessel and the probe. This allows the probe to function as a depth-based sensing unit while the boat-side ESP32 and Arduino Mega receive, organize, and relay the data back to the operator. Overall, this system gives AquaScan one central power source and one coordinated electronics platform for propulsion, probe communication, and live data collection.  Propulsion and Cooling System  Motor and Drive Layout:  Each pontoon contains one 1100 kv brushless motor mounted inside the hull. These motors drive external propellers through rotating shafts, allowing AquaScan to generate thrust in the water while keeping the motors protected inside the vessel. This setup was selected because it is better suited for a low-speed, high-payload platform rather than a more speed-oriented propulsion setup. It also made the system easier to waterproof and integrate into the printed hull structure. AquaScan uses CNC metal propellers selected for high thrust efficiency and durability, allowing the vessel to move a heavy load more effectively while maintaining more consistent performance under sustained operation.  Shaft Sealing and Power Transfer:  Because the motors are not directly submerged, each drive shaft passes through a grease-lubricated stuffing tube. This shaft system allows rotational power to transfer from the dry interior of the hull to the external propeller while helping

12  Probe and Tether Design:  The probe was designed to sink rather than float, keeping the tether in tension to reduce slack and improve depth consistency. Its shape was refined to reduce drag during lowering and retrieval. The probe connects to the vessel through a marine cable that serves as both the deployment tether and the power/signal link. The cable wraps cleanly onto the spool without tangling, eliminating the need for a separate detangling mechanism. A slip ring maintains continuous electrical connection during spool rotation, preventing cable twisting and reducing torque transfer into the system.  Depth Positioning:  Probe depth is estimated from calibrated spool rotation. As the deployment motor rotates the spool, the length of cable released can be approximated from spool rotation, allowing the probe to be positioned at selected depths. The spool was designed long enough that the cable wraps without significantly increasing the radius, keeping the arc length released per rotation nearly constant.  ●   Rotation-based depth estimate:   spool rotation is used to approximate tether length deployed  ●   Long spool geometry:   keeps spool radius nearly constant during deployment  ●   Slip ring connection:   maintains wire continuity while allowing rotation  ●   Tensioned tether:   the sinking probe helps keep the line straight and reduces slack  ●   Stationary depth sampling:   the vessel is intended to stop at each monitoring point before deploying the probe, improving depth control and measurement consistency  ●   Worm gear retention:   provides a self-locking effect to help prevent the probe from freely dropping if power is lost  ●   Balanced component placement:   positions the winch/probe assembly opposite the battery to improve weight distribution across the platform  Probe Design and Sensor Integration  The probe is the scientific core of AquaScan and the part of the system most directly responsible for solving the monitoring problem defined in this project. While the surface vessel provides mobility, positioning, and deployment, the probe is the part that actually collects the depth-based environmental data. This makes the probe essential to AquaScan’s value as a monitoring system because it turns the vessel from a mobile platform into an active scientific measurement tool.  Probe Design:  The probe body is approximately 16 in long and 3 in in diameter and was manufactured from PETG as a multi-part 3D printed assembly. Its shape was designed to reduce drag during deployment, transit, and retrieval, with fins that help keep the probe more stable and aligned as it moves through the water. The probe was also designed to sink rather than float, which keeps the tether in tension and improves consistency in depth positioning.

Scientific Sensor Suite:  The probe was designed as a multi-parameter water-quality sensing package, allowing AquaScan to collect several related measurements at the same location and depth. A stronger scientific picture comes from comparing several conditions together and seeing how they change through the water column.  1.   Dissolved Oxygen Sensor  Dissolved oxygen is one of the most important measurements in the system because it directly affects whether aquatic organisms can survive in a given part of the water column. Oxygen levels can drop significantly at deeper depths, especially when the water becomes stratified, meaning it separates into layers that do not mix easily. When that happens, deeper water may receive less oxygen from the surface, creating low-oxygen bottom waters that place stress on fish and other aquatic life. By measuring dissolved oxygen at selected depths, AquaScan can detect these hidden subsurface conditions much more effectively than a surface-only system.  2.   Turbidity Sensor  Turbidity measures how cloudy the water is due to suspended particles such as sediment, organic matter, or other material in the water. This is scientifically useful because increased turbidity can reduce light penetration, affect aquatic plant growth, and indicate runoff, erosion, or other disturbances. Measuring turbidity at depth helps show whether suspended material is concentrated near the surface or persists lower in the water column as well.  3.   Total Dissolved Solids Sensor  Total dissolved solids indicate how much dissolved material is present in the water. This helps reveal changes in water composition that may be linked to runoff, nutrient input, or other environmental influences. While it is not usually interpreted by itself, it becomes much more useful when compared with the other probe measurements at the same depth.  4.   Temperature Sensor  Temperature is important because it helps reveal thermal layering in the water column. Even if temperature changes are not always dramatic, they still matter because temperature affects oxygen availability, biological activity, and the way water layers mix. In AquaScan, temperature helps provide the physical context needed to interpret the rest of the measurements more accurately.  5.   pH Sensor  pH measures how acidic or basic the water is. This matters because aquatic organisms often depend on a relatively stable chemical environment, and unusual pH values can indicate pollution, chemical imbalance, or biological activity that may be

14  affecting water quality. When measured alongside dissolved oxygen, turbidity, and other values, pH helps provide a more complete picture of aquatic conditions.  6.   Light Sensor and Ultraviolet Sensor  The light and ultraviolet sensors help measure how light conditions change below the surface. These sensors are positioned behind a clear PLA front cap so outside light can still reach them while the electronics remain enclosed and dry. This is useful because light penetration affects underwater visibility and can also indicate whether suspended material or other water conditions are limiting how deeply light can travel.  7.   Ultrasonic Range Sensor  The ultrasonic range sensor is used to detect proximity to the bottom so the probe can avoid contacting the floor in shallow areas. This improves deployment safety, but it also helps the system collect more controlled depth-based measurements in places where bottom clearance may be limited. Since AquaScan is intended for shallow, sheltered, and nearshore waters, that makes the probe more practical in the exact environments it was designed to study.  8.   Conductivity Sensor  Conductivity measures how well the water can carry an electrical current, which depends on the amount of dissolved ions such as salts and minerals present. This is useful because changes in conductivity can indicate variations in water composition, including runoff, pollution, or mixing between different water sources. For example, higher conductivity may suggest increased dissolved material from runoff or human activity, while lower values may indicate cleaner or more diluted water. When measured alongside total dissolved solids and other sensors at different depths, conductivity helps provide additional context about how water chemistry changes throughout the water column.  Control and Data Output Software  AquaScan was designed as a semi-autonomous monitoring platform, meaning it can be operated manually when direct control is needed or guided to selected sampling locations using GPS-based navigation. The system is managed through a Unity interface, which acts as the main operator control and visualization system. This interface functions like a live digital representation of the vessel and its mission, allowing the user to control the boat, place sampling points, monitor system behavior, and view scientific data through one program. This makes AquaScan more than a remotely controlled vessel. It becomes a complete data-collection platform that connects navigation, sampling, and visualization into one workflow.  Manual Control Through the Unity Interface  In manual mode, the operator drives AquaScan using a virtual joystick inside the Unity interface. The joystick uses an X-value for turning and a Y-value for forward or reverse motion. The software first applies a dead zone, which prevents small accidental joystick movements from affecting the vessel. This is important because it keeps the controls stable and avoids unwanted motion when the operator is trying to hold position or make fine adjustments.  After the joystick input is cleaned, the system uses differential drive mixing to convert one joystick input into two motor outputs:  ●   Left motor output = Y + X  ●   Right motor output = Y - X  This allows the vessel to move forward and turn smoothly using a single control input. Once the left and right motor values are calculated, the system converts them into ESC pulse commands. Neutral is mapped to 1500 μs, forward throttle increases toward 2000 μs, and reverse throttle decreases toward 1000 μs. These commands are sent from the Unity interface over Wi-Fi to the onboard ESP32,


===== PAGE 16 =====
15  then passed to the Arduino Mega, which outputs the final control signals to the ESCs and motors. This process makes the system predictable because the operator input is consistently translated into vessel movement.  Manual control features include:  ●   Virtual joystick steering and throttle control  ●   Dead-zone filtering for more stable handling  ●   Differential drive mixing for two-motor steering  ●   Real-time Wi-Fi communication between interface and vessel  ●   Live motor-output feedback during operation  Semi-Autonomous Navigation  AquaScan also supports semi-autonomous operation through its GPS module and Unity-based mission interface. Instead of driving entirely by hand, the operator can select specific GPS sampling points within the Unity interface. Once those locations are chosen, the software compares the boat’s current position to the target coordinates and adjusts motor output so the vessel moves toward the correct point.  This makes the system especially useful for repeated environmental monitoring because it allows sampling to occur at more controlled and consistent locations. That matters in scientific fieldwork because repeated measurements are much more useful when they are taken at known positions rather than at random or approximate locations.  The operator can still use manual control when needed, so the system is not locked into one mode. This gives AquaScan flexibility in real field conditions, where obstacles, wind, or changing water conditions may require direct intervention.  Semi-autonomous navigation allows AquaScan to:  ●   Travel to selected GPS waypoints  ●   Pause at specific monitoring locations  ●   Support repeatable sampling at known coordinates  ●   Combine autonomous guidance with manual override when needed  Sampling Process  Once AquaScan reaches a sampling location, the vessel is intended to stop and hold position while the probe is lowered to selected depths. This allows the system to position the probe at selected depths without relying on a pressure sensor. This lets AquaScan build a vertical profile of the water column rather than collecting only surface measurements.

This is one of the most important advantages of the selected solution because the deployable probe allows the vessel to collect below-surface information without requiring a fixed buoy station or manual depth sampling.  The location-based results also supported the value of the system. The pond heat maps showed that water-quality conditions varied across the testing area rather than remaining consistent from point to point. This means that a single sample would not fully

This supports the idea that mobile monitoring can provide more useful environmental data than relying only on fixed sensors or isolated manual samples.  The data output itself was another important result. AquaScan did not only collect raw sensor readings; it organized them into tables, graphs, and spatial visualizations. 27  IV. Completed Refinements and Issues  After testing and development, AquaScan’s final prototype included several refinements that improved waterproofing, structure, propulsion, probe stability, and data collection. These refinements were made in response to design limitations that appeared during planning, construction, and early testing.  1.   Waterproofing Strategy  ●   Issue:   AquaScan contains sensitive electronics, sensors, motors, and wiring, so the vessel and probe had to be protected from water exposure. Although PETG is relatively water-resistant, 3D printed parts can still allow moisture through layer lines, seams, caps, and sensor openings.  ●   Refinement made:   The team used a multi-layer waterproofing strategy instead of relying on 3D printed parts alone. The probe used O-ring seals at the main cap and tail connections, with screws tightening the parts together to compress the seal. Sensor openings on the probe were sealed using silicone sealant, while the vessel hulls were reinforced with epoxy resin, JB Weld, and sealed lid interfaces where needed.  ●   Result:   This refinement gave AquaScan a more reliable sealing system for both the vessel and probe. The O-ring test pieces held water for approximately 12 hours without visible leakage, and the probe remained sealed during pool testing.  2.   Structural Support and Weight Distribution  ●   Issue:   AquaScan had to support a heavy battery, electronics tray, probe deployment system, and propulsion hardware without flexing or becoming unbalanced in the water.  ●   Refinement made:   The hull design included internal pontoon ribs to increase stiffness without adding excessive weight. The electronics tray was supported using carbon fiber rods and truss-style reinforcement between the pontoons. Heavy components were also placed intentionally, with the battery positioned to help balance the weight of the winch and other systems instead of concentrating all major loads on one side.  ●   Result:   These refinements improved the vessel’s stiffness, balance, and loaded stability. During flotation testing, the full vessel floated with the loaded waterline at approximately 3.5 in up the 7 in pontoon height.  3.   Propulsion and Motor Waterproofing  ●   Issue:   The original propulsion plan assumed the motors could be mounted more directly around water exposure, but further research showed that the motors were not intended to be fully submerged. This created a major waterproofing issue because the motors still needed to drive external propellers.  ●   Refinement made:   The design was changed to use an internal motor and stuffing tube system. This allowed the motors to stay protected inside the hull while the shaft passed through the pontoon to drive the external propeller. Marine grease inside the stuffing tube helped reduce friction while also creating a water-resistant seal around the rotating shaft.  ●   Result:   This refinement improved the design because it protected the motors, reduced the risk of water entering the hull, and created a more realistic marine propulsion layout. The propulsion system could then be tested in the pool without placing the motors directly in the water.  4.   Motor Cooling System  ●   Issue:   Because AquaScan uses motors under sustained load, overheating was a potential issue during longer operation. Even though the motors did not overheat during testing, the team wanted to reduce the risk before extended field use.  ●   Refinement made:   A passive water-cooling system was added to the propulsion layout. A tube was routed through the pontoon so that water could pass near the motor cooling jacket and exit toward the rear of the vessel.


===== PAGE 29 =====
28  ●   Result:   This refinement improved reliability by adding a simple cooling path without requiring an extra pump. It kept the system mechanically simple while still addressing the risk of heat buildup during longer runs.  5.   Probe Deployment and Depth Stability  ●   Issue:   The probe had to lower into the water column without excessive drag, swinging, or floating upward. It also needed to stay relatively vertical so depth-based readings would be more consistent.  ●   Refinement made:   The probe body was designed with stabilizing fins to reduce unwanted movement through the water. Additional internal weight was used so the probe would sink instead of floating near the surface. The spool was also designed with a wider cable-wrapping surface to reduce tangling and limit how many times the tether had to wrap around itself.  ●   Result:   These refinements improved probe deployment, retrieval, and underwater stability. However, depth measurement remains a limitation because the current system estimates depth from tether deployment rather than measuring it directly at the probe. A future version would improve this by adding a pressure-based depth sensor.  6.   Light and UV Sensor Accuracy Issue  ●   Issue:   The light and UV sensors did not produce readings as accurately as expected because the clear printed probe material was not fully transparent. The printed part came out more frosted than clear, which diffused the light before it reached the sensors.  ●   Refinement planned:   A future version would replace the printed clear section with a true optical window made from a clearer material, such as acrylic or polycarbonate. Instead of printing the entire cap in clear filament, the probe could use a smaller sealed window directly over the light and UV sensors.  ●   Result:   This issue showed that material choice affects sensor accuracy, especially for sensors that depend on light transmission. The rest of the probe could remain printed and sealed, but light-based sensors need a clearer viewing path to produce more reliable readings.

29  Communication of Solution  AquaScan addresses the annual Engineering Design theme, “Engineering the Tools of Scientific Discovery,” by improving how water-quality data can be collected, organized, and interpreted in real aquatic environments. Scientific discovery depends on tools that make hidden conditions measurable. In water-quality research, many important conditions cannot be understood just by looking at the surface. Dissolved oxygen, turbidity, temperature, pH, dissolved solids, and light availability can all change across a body of water and through the water column. AquaScan was designed as a scientific monitoring tool that helps reveal those changes through direct, sensor-based measurement.  The main purpose of AquaScan is to make aquatic data collection more useful for research. Instead of only collecting surface readings or requiring repeated manual sampling, AquaScan uses a semi-autonomous vessel with a deployable multi-parameter probe. The probe allows readings to be taken below the surface at selected depths, which gives researchers a clearer understanding of how water conditions change vertically. This is important because issues such as stratification, low-oxygen bottom waters, sediment movement, runoff influence, and reduced light penetration may not be visible from surface-level information alone. By collecting depth-aware data, AquaScan provides a stronger way to study the conditions that affect aquatic ecosystems.  AquaScan also supports scientific discovery by connecting each measurement to context. A water-quality reading is more valuable when researchers know where it was taken and how deep it was taken from. By linking sensor values with GPS position and probe depth, AquaScan turns separate readings into organized environmental data. This allows patterns to be compared across different locations and depths, making the information more useful for analysis. The system can help researchers identify problem areas, compare changes between sampling points, and better understand how conditions vary within a body of water.  The data output is another major part of AquaScan’s connection to the theme. Scientific tools are not only useful because they collect information, but because they make that information easier to understand. AquaScan’s interface and digital output are designed to display collected measurements through maps, graphs, heat maps, and layered depth-based views. These formats help turn raw sensor readings into visual information that can support research, restoration planning, and environmental decision-making. This makes AquaScan more than a mobile sensing platform. It becomes a complete tool for collecting and communicating scientific data.  The connection to the theme is strongest in the way AquaScan improves the research process:  ●   Direct scientific measurement:   The probe collects water-quality data using multiple sensors instead of relying only on visual observation or surface assumptions.  ●   Depth-aware data collection:   Measurements can be taken below the surface, allowing researchers to study changes through the water column.  ●   Location-based sampling:   GPS-linked readings make it easier to compare conditions across different areas of a water body.  ●   Improved data interpretation: Graphs, maps, heat maps, and depth profiles help make collected data easier to understand and use.  ●   Environmental research support:   The system can help identify conditions related to oxygen stress, turbidity, runoff, chemical imbalance, and limited light penetration.  ●   Practical field use:   AquaScan is designed to make detailed water-quality monitoring more accessible for researchers, restoration groups, and environmental organizations.  Overall, AquaScan communicates the theme by showing how engineering can expand what scientists are able to observe and measure. By combining direct sensing, depth-based sampling, location tracking, and visual data output, AquaScan provides a practical tool for studying aquatic environments more clearly. The system supports scientific discovery by helping researchers gather stronger data, recognize environmental patterns, and make better-informed decisions about the health of water bodies.
