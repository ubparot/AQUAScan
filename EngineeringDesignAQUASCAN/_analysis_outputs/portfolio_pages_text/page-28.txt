27  IV. Completed Refinements and Issues  After testing and development, AquaScan’s final prototype included several refinements that improved waterproofing, structure, propulsion, probe stability, and data collection. These refinements were made in response to design limitations that appeared during planning, construction, and early testing.  1.   Waterproofing Strategy  ●   Issue:   AquaScan contains sensitive electronics, sensors, motors, and wiring, so the vessel and probe had to be protected from water exposure. Although PETG is relatively water-resistant, 3D printed parts can still allow moisture through layer lines, seams, caps, and sensor openings.  ●   Refinement made:   The team used a multi-layer waterproofing strategy instead of relying on 3D printed parts alone. The probe used O-ring seals at the main cap and tail connections, with screws tightening the parts together to compress the seal. Sensor openings on the probe were sealed using silicone sealant, while the vessel hulls were reinforced with epoxy resin, JB Weld, and sealed lid interfaces where needed.  ●   Result:   This refinement gave AquaScan a more reliable sealing system for both the vessel and probe. The O-ring test pieces held water for approximately 12 hours without visible leakage, and the probe remained sealed during pool testing.  2.   Structural Support and Weight Distribution  ●   Issue:   AquaScan had to support a heavy battery, electronics tray, probe deployment system, and propulsion hardware without flexing or becoming unbalanced in the water.  ●   Refinement made:   The hull design included internal pontoon ribs to increase stiffness without adding excessive weight. The electronics tray was supported using carbon fiber rods and truss-style reinforcement between the pontoons. Heavy components were also placed intentionally, with the battery positioned to help balance the weight of the winch and other systems instead of concentrating all major loads on one side.  ●   Result:   These refinements improved the vessel’s stiffness, balance, and loaded stability. During flotation testing, the full vessel floated with the loaded waterline at approximately 3.5 in up the 7 in pontoon height.  3.   Propulsion and Motor Waterproofing  ●   Issue:   The original propulsion plan assumed the motors could be mounted more directly around water exposure, but further research showed that the motors were not intended to be fully submerged. This created a major waterproofing issue because the motors still needed to drive external propellers.  ●   Refinement made:   The design was changed to use an internal motor and stuffing tube system. This allowed the motors to stay protected inside the hull while the shaft passed through the pontoon to drive the external propeller. Marine grease inside the stuffing tube helped reduce friction while also creating a water-resistant seal around the rotating shaft.  ●   Result:   This refinement improved the design because it protected the motors, reduced the risk of water entering the hull, and created a more realistic marine propulsion layout. The propulsion system could then be tested in the pool without placing the motors directly in the water.  4.   Motor Cooling System  ●   Issue:   Because AquaScan uses motors under sustained load, overheating was a potential issue during longer operation. Even though the motors did not overheat during testing, the team wanted to reduce the risk before extended field use.  ●   Refinement made:   A passive water-cooling system was added to the propulsion layout. A tube was routed through the pontoon so that water could pass near the motor cooling jacket and exit toward the rear of the vessel.
