What This Data Can Reveal  1. Stratification and low-oxygen bottom waters  One of the most important scientific uses of AquaScan data is identifying stratification, which occurs when the water separates into layers that do not mix easily. When this happens, deeper water can become isolated from oxygen entering near the surface. Dissolved oxygen is critical because it represents the oxygen available to aquatic organisms, and low dissolved-oxygen conditions can stress or kill aquatic life. Water temperature is closely tied to this process, since warmer water holds less dissolved oxygen than cooler water. By collecting dissolved oxygen and temperature data at multiple depths, AquaScan can help reveal stratified conditions and detect low-oxygen bottom waters that surface measurements alone may miss.  2. Areas affected by runoff, sediment, and suspended material  AquaScan can also help identify regions influenced by runoff, erosion, or suspended particles. Turbidity is a measure of how much material in the water reduces clarity by absorbing and scattering light. High turbidity can indicate sediment input, organic matter, algae, or other suspended material. This matters scientifically because reduced water clarity limits light penetration, lowers photosynthesis, and can affect oxygen production and habitat quality. Suspended material can also damage aquatic organisms directly by clogging fish gills, affecting egg and larval development, and smothering benthic habitats as particles settle. Because AquaScan connects turbidity readings to exact locations and depths, it can help researchers identify where these conditions are strongest and whether they are concentrated near the surface or remain deeper in the water column.  3. Chemical stress and changes in water composition  The pH and total dissolved solids measurements collected by AquaScan can help researchers recognize chemical changes in the water. pH is important because it affects the solubility and biological availability of nutrients and metals, meaning shifts in pH can change how aquatic life experiences the surrounding environment. Unusual pH values may indicate pollution, runoff, or broader chemical imbalance. Total dissolved solids provide a broader indication of how much dissolved material is present in the water, which can help show changes in composition between sampling locations or across depths. When these measurements are interpreted alongside dissolved oxygen, turbidity, and temperature, they provide a more complete view of whether the water is chemically stable or experiencing stress.  4. Light limitation and underwater productivity  AquaScan’s light and ultraviolet measurements can help show how much light is reaching different depths.
