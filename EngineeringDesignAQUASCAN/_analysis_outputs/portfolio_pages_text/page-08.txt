7  Selection of Final Solution: COMPARISON OF SOLUTIONS:  Advantages   Disadvantages  Solution 1:   ●   Depth-based data collection:  Multi-depth sensors can monitor how conditions change through the water column instead of only at the surface, making it easier to detect vertical stratification, low-oxygen bottom waters, and other below-surface variations that surface readings may miss.  ●   Reliable fixed-location data:  Anchored stations can provide consistent measurements from the same locations over time.  ●   Strong long-term trend tracking:  Continuous monitoring helps identify recurring environmental patterns and below-surface stress.  ●   High installation cost:   Building and maintaining a full buoy network would require significant equipment, anchoring hardware, and upkeep.  ●   Limited flexibility : Each station only represents one location, so monitoring a new area would require deploying additional stations.  ●   Difficult to scale widely : Covering a large region with enough buoys to create detailed data would be expensive and labor-intensive.  Solution 2:   ●   Rapid large-area assessment:  Drones can survey broad sections of water in a short period of time, improving spatial coverage.  ●   Access to difficult regions:   They can observe shallow, sheltered, or hard-to-reach areas without deploying monitoring platforms directly in the water.  ●   Hotspot identification:   Drone imaging can help locate stress areas such as sediment plumes, algae buildup, discoloration, or runoff zones, supporting targeted follow-up monitoring.  ●   Limited flight endurance:   Battery life and payload capacity restrict how long drones can operate and how much monitoring hardware they can carry.  ●   Less suited for repeated close-point sampling:   Drone systems are better for quick checks and targeted spot measurements than for dense, repeated monitoring.  ●   High Expense:   Drone-based monitoring can require expensive aircraft, specialized imaging attachments, and repeated flights, making long-term use more costly and technically demanding.  Solution 3:   ●   Direct sensor-based measurement:  The vessel carries sensors directly on the water, allowing it to collect on-site environmental data.  ●   Detailed small-area coverage:   It can collect measurements from many nearby points, making fine-scale local changes easier to detect.  ●   Effective in nearshore areas:   It can monitor marinas, coves, shoreline zones, and other sheltered environments more easily than larger survey boats .  ●   Surface-confined sensing:   A surface-only USV cannot sense vertical gradients in water quality, so it may miss stratification, hypoxic bottom waters, or other subsurface variability.  ●   Lower efficiency over large survey areas:   Because a USV relies on route-based point sampling, achieving high spatial resolution across a large body of water can require significant travel time and energy consumption.
