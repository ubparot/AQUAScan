20  Engineering Design Process of the Creation of the Model  Steps   Reasoning  Defining the Problem   We began by reviewing the annual theme, “Engineering the Tools of Scientific Discovery,” and interpreting it as a challenge focused on measurement, observation, and data collection. This led us toward water-quality monitoring because it gave us a clear way to design a tool that could support scientific research and sustainability.  Researching and Gathering Information  After choosing water-quality monitoring as the general direction, we researched gaps in current monitoring methods. This included research into aquatic sensor systems, depth-based water-quality data, manual sampling, fixed monitoring platforms, and the way conditions can change through water bodies. We also researched measurements such as dissolved oxygen, turbidity, temperature, pH, total dissolved solids, and light penetration to understand what data would be most useful.  Brainstorming and Selecting the Solution  Once the problem was clearer, we focused on a design that could improve current monitoring methods without becoming too large or impractical. Our brainstorming centered on direct sensor measurement, mobility, controlled depth sampling, and digital data output. This led to AquaScan: a semi-autonomous surface vessel with a deployable multi-sensor probe that collects readings at selected locations and depths.  Designing and Planning the Prototype  After selecting AquaScan, most of the planning went into CAD design, parts research, electronics layout, and tolerance checks. The vessel, pontoons, electronics tray, probe, deployment system, and sensor layout all had to match the real dimensions of the components being used. We also had to account for waterproofing, buoyancy, weight distribution, motor placement, tether routing, and how the probe would move through the water during sampling.  Building and Testing the Prototype  The prototype was built by 3D printing all structural parts, assembling the pontoon and tray system, installing the electronics and mechanical systems, and waterproofing the build with epoxy resin and sealing methods. Testing began in a pool where the environment was easier to control. There, we checked the water seals, flotation, sensor readings, movement, and probe deployment before moving to a real water body. We then tested in a small lake which allowed us to observe the system in a more realistic environment, collect water-quality data, and practice a full monitoring run using navigation, probe deployment, sensor collection, and data output.  Evaluating, Iterating, and Communicating the Solution  After testing, the results were used to evaluate what worked, what failed, and what changes needed to be made. The main areas for improvement included waterproofing, mechanical reliability, autonomy, system efficiency, and clearer data output through the software interface. Communication was also part of the process through outreach to MI Technical Solutions, where we explained AquaScan, received maritime engineering feedback, and connected the project to real-world environmental monitoring. The final solution was communicated through the portfolio, prototype/model, display, and presentation.
