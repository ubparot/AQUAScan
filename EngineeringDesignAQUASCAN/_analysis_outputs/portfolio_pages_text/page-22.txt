21  I. Narrative Testing  AquaScan was evaluated through a staged testing process that moved from controlled subsystem checks to full in-water operation. Because the prototype combines electrical systems, waterproof structures, propulsion, a deployable probe, sensors, and software output, the team tested lower-risk components first before placing the full vessel into a real aquatic environment.  Stage 1: Controlled Subsystem Testing  The first stage focused on verifying AquaScan’s basic functions before full vessel testing. This included electronics, sensor readings, motor function, waterproofing, buoyancy, and individual component checks.  Purpose of the test:   Confirm that the main electronic, sensing, mechanical, and structural systems worked before they were integrated into the full vessel.  Electronics, sensor, and mechanical function testing:  ●   Individual sensors were connected to the Arduino/ESP32 control system and tested in controlled water samples before being permanently installed into the probe. This verified that each sensor produced readable values and helped the team confirm basic wiring, signal output, and sensor response in water.  ●   Motor connections, ESC outputs, and control signals were tested before installation in the vessel. The team checked that the motors responded to the correct inputs and that the propulsion system could be activated safely before placing the full boat or sealed electronics at risk.  ●   This stage also helped establish AquaScan’s software workflow. By testing sensor readings in a controlled environment first, the team could begin receiving values, organizing them digitally, exporting data, and converting the results into graphs and visual outputs for later pool and pond testing.  Waterproofing and buoyancy testing:  ●   Waterproofing was tested progressively instead of all at once. The team first tested smaller printed seal pieces with O-rings to determine whether the seal geometry and compression were strong enough to hold water. After several variations, the selected O-ring setup held water for approximately 12 hours without leakage. This gave the team confidence before applying the seal design to larger vessel and probe components.  ●   Larger component tests were then completed using the pontoon and probe. First, a single pontoon was placed in the pool to evaluate flotation, waterline behavior, and watertightness before motors or electronics were installed.  ●   The probe was also placed in water after the sensors were sealed into the body. This allowed the team to check the silicone-sealed sensor openings and confirm that the probe could collect readings while exposed to water.  ●   After the individual tests, the full vessel was assembled and placed in the pool for an initial buoyancy check. The boat was first tested without the battery to reduce risk, then tested again with the battery installed to confirm that the full operating load still floated correctly and maintained stable balance. The loaded waterline was approximately 3.5 in up the 7 in pontoon height.  What this stage showed:   Stage 1 confirmed that AquaScan’s major components were ready for full-system testing. The sensors produced readable values, the motors and ESCs responded correctly, the O-ring seal design was reliable in controlled testing, the pontoon floated as expected, and the probe remained watertight during initial submersion. This stage reduced risk before the full vessel was assembled and placed in the pool with the battery and electronics installed.
