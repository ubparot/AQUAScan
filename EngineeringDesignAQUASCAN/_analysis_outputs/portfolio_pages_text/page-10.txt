9  Structural Design and Material Selection  AquaScan was structurally designed to support a heavy scientific payload while remaining stable, waterproof, and compact enough for nearshore and sheltered environments. Because the vessel carries a large battery, onboard electronics, motors, and a deployable probe, the hull and support structure had to be designed around flotation, reinforcement, sealing, and balance rather than speed alone.  Hull Configuration  The vessel uses a dual-pontoon layout as its primary flotation system. This gave AquaScan a wider base, improved stability, and created open space between the hulls for the probe deployment mechanism.  ●   Dual pontoons:   increase stability and distribute buoyancy across both sides of the vessel  ●   Pontoon dimensions:   ~ 42” long × 7” tall × 6" wide  ●   Estimated loaded draft:   3.5” of pontoon height submerged under operating load  ●   Open center span:   allows the probe to deploy vertically without interfering with the propulsion system  ●   Survey-oriented geometry : prioritizes control, balance, and payload support  Structural Support and Materials  The central tray spans between the two pontoons and supports the battery and electronics, so this area required additional strength. All major parts were 3D printed in PETG, since PETG provided a strong balance of durability, water resistance, printability, and cost for a functional aquatic prototype. The pontoons also include internal ribbing, which increases hull stiffness, helps prevent flexing across the long printed sections, and provides additional support without adding excessive weight. To keep the structure rigid under load, the tray was supported with printed truss features and reinforced using carbon fiber rods. This was especially important because the battery alone weighs ~23 lb, making the center structure one of the most heavily loaded areas of the vessel.  Waterproofing Strategy  Because AquaScan operates directly in water, sealing was a major design requirement. Although PETG is relatively water-resistant, 3D printed parts can still allow leakage through seams and layer lines. To solve this, the printed components were joined using plastic-welding methods, with marine-grade JB Weld added at key seams and connection points for additional bonding and sealing. After assembly, the hull was coated with epoxy resin as a final waterproofing layer.  ●   O-ring seals: used in removable lids, wire pass-throughs, and probe interfaces  ●   JB Weld reinforcement: adds bonding and sealing at key hull connections  ●   Silicone sealant: used for more permanent seals around sensor openings  ●   Epoxy coating: improves sealing and reduces permeability through print seams
