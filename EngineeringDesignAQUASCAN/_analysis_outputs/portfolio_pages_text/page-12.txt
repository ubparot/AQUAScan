Each motor is fitted with a cooling jacket, and tubing is routed through the pontoon so that forward motion pushes water into the system, across the motor jacket, and back out of the hull. This was important because the motors operate under sustained load and can generate significant heat during longer runs, increasing the risk of overheating without additional cooling. By using the surrounding water as a cooling source, the system improves reliability without requiring a separate pump.  ●   Water-cooling jacket:   removes heat from the motor during sustained operation  ●   Forward-flow tubing:   uses vessel motion to move water through the cooling loop  ●   Passive cooling layout:   improves thermal control while keeping the system simple  Deployment and Depth-Control System  One of AquaScan’s most distinctive features is its ability to collect depth-based water-quality data. This directly addresses a major limitation in aquatic monitoring: most mobile vessels collect only surface measurements, and the few systems that do monitor multiple depths are primarily stationary. AquaScan was designed to overcome that gap by combining waypoint-based mobility with a deployable probe capable of sampling at selected depths.  Deployment Mechanism:  The probe is raised and lowered using a high-torque motor and spool system mounted on the vessel. This was necessary because the probe contains multiple sensors and internal electronics, creating enough weight and drag that a smaller motor would not allow for reliable deployment and retrieval.  ●   High-torque motor:   provides enough force to reel the probe in and out under load  ●   Spool-based deployment:   allows controlled tether payout and retrieval  ●   Vertical deployment path:   lowers the probe between the pontoons for cleaner movement through the water column
