Reduced light penetration, especially when paired with higher turbidity, can point to conditions that limit plant growth and reduce ecological productivity. Depth-based light data can therefore help researchers understand not only whether the water is cloudy, but how those conditions affect the underwater environment.  How This Data Supports Research and Restoration  ●   Improved environmental assessment:   AquaScan collects multiple water-quality measurements across different locations and depths, allowing researchers to compare conditions more precisely and recognize relationships between factors such as temperature, turbidity, dissolved oxygen, and dissolved solids.  ●   More targeted restoration:   Because each reading is connected to a specific GPS sampling point and probe depth, AquaScan can help identify localized problem areas instead of treating an entire water body as one uniform condition. This supports more focused follow-up testing, restoration planning, and resource use.  ●   Clearer communication of results:   AquaScan data can be converted into maps, graphs, heat maps, and depth profiles, making the results easier to interpret visually. This helps researchers, restoration groups, and community stakeholders understand where conditions are improving, worsening, or requiring further investigation.  Advancements Beyond Current Monitoring Technology  To improve AquaScan’s effectiveness in water-quality monitoring, we combined several features that are not often found together in one compact system.  1.   Depth-based monitoring on a small mobile platform:  Many smaller USVs are mainly limited to surface measurements. AquaScan improves on that by using a deployable probe system that allows measurements to be collected at selected depths, making it possible to identify changes through the water column.  2.   Multiple measurements collected at the same point:  Instead of relying on one sensor or one condition at a time, AquaScan collects dissolved oxygen, turbidity, temperature, pH, dissolved solids, and light-related data together. This gives researchers a more complete set of measurements to compare at each sampling point.  3.   GPS-based sampling with organized data output:  AquaScan improves on standard monitoring by connecting each reading to a specific sampling location and depth. This makes the collected data more useful for various visual outputs that help researchers compare conditions across an area.  4.   Improved use in sheltered and shallow environments:  Larger survey systems can be less practical in marinas, coves, shoreline zones, and other smaller aquatic areas. AquaScan was designed specifically for these environments, allowing detailed monitoring in places that are often harder to study with larger equipment.  5.   Sampling, visualization, and interpretation in one system:  AquaScan was designed not only to collect measurements, but also to organize them in a clearer format through the Unity interface and CSV output. This allows the data to be reviewed, compared, and interpreted more efficiently through maps, graphs, and other visual outputs that many current systems do not provide.
