Light Sensor and Ultraviolet Sensor  The light and ultraviolet sensors help measure how light conditions change below the surface. These sensors are positioned behind a clear PLA front cap so outside light can still reach them while the electronics remain enclosed and dry. This is useful because light penetration affects underwater visibility and can also indicate whether suspended material or other water conditions are limiting how deeply light can travel.  7.   Ultrasonic Range Sensor  The ultrasonic range sensor is used to detect proximity to the bottom so the probe can avoid contacting the floor in shallow areas. This improves deployment safety, but it also helps the system collect more controlled depth-based measurements in places where bottom clearance may be limited. Since AquaScan is intended for shallow, sheltered, and nearshore waters, that makes the probe more practical in the exact environments it was designed to study.  8.   Conductivity Sensor  Conductivity measures how well the water can carry an electrical current, which depends on the amount of dissolved ions such as salts and minerals present. This is useful because changes in conductivity can indicate variations in water composition, including runoff, pollution, or mixing between different water sources. For example, higher conductivity may suggest increased dissolved material from runoff or human activity, while lower values may indicate cleaner or more diluted water. When measured alongside total dissolved solids and other sensors at different depths, conductivity helps provide additional context about how water chemistry changes throughout the water column.  Control and Data Output Software  AquaScan was designed as a semi-autonomous monitoring platform, meaning it can be operated manually when direct control is needed or guided to selected sampling locations using GPS-based navigation. The system is managed through a Unity interface, which acts as the main operator control and visualization system. This interface functions like a live digital representation of the vessel and its mission, allowing the user to control the boat, place sampling points, monitor system behavior, and view scientific data through one program. This makes AquaScan more than a remotely controlled vessel. It becomes a complete data-collection platform that connects navigation, sampling, and visualization into one workflow.  Manual Control Through the Unity Interface  In manual mode, the operator drives AquaScan using a virtual joystick inside the Unity interface. The joystick uses an X-value for turning and a Y-value for forward or reverse motion. The software first applies a dead zone, which prevents small accidental joystick movements from affecting the vessel. This is important because it keeps the controls stable and avoids unwanted motion when the operator is trying to hold position or make fine adjustments.  After the joystick input is cleaned, the system uses differential drive mixing to convert one joystick input into two motor outputs:  ●   Left motor output = Y + X  ●   Right motor output = Y - X  This allows the vessel to move forward and turn smoothly using a single control input. Once the left and right motor values are calculated, the system converts them into ESC pulse commands. Neutral is mapped to 1500 μs, forward throttle increases toward 2000 μs, and reverse throttle decreases toward 1000 μs.
