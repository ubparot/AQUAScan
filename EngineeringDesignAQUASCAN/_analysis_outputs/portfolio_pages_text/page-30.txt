29  Communication of Solution  AquaScan addresses the annual Engineering Design theme, “Engineering the Tools of Scientific Discovery,” by improving how water-quality data can be collected, organized, and interpreted in real aquatic environments. Scientific discovery depends on tools that make hidden conditions measurable. In water-quality research, many important conditions cannot be understood just by looking at the surface. Dissolved oxygen, turbidity, temperature, pH, dissolved solids, and light availability can all change across a body of water and through the water column. AquaScan was designed as a scientific monitoring tool that helps reveal those changes through direct, sensor-based measurement.  The main purpose of AquaScan is to make aquatic data collection more useful for research. Instead of only collecting surface readings or requiring repeated manual sampling, AquaScan uses a semi-autonomous vessel with a deployable multi-parameter probe. The probe allows readings to be taken below the surface at selected depths, which gives researchers a clearer understanding of how water conditions change vertically. This is important because issues such as stratification, low-oxygen bottom waters, sediment movement, runoff influence, and reduced light penetration may not be visible from surface-level information alone. By collecting depth-aware data, AquaScan provides a stronger way to study the conditions that affect aquatic ecosystems.  AquaScan also supports scientific discovery by connecting each measurement to context. A water-quality reading is more valuable when researchers know where it was taken and how deep it was taken from. By linking sensor values with GPS position and probe depth, AquaScan turns separate readings into organized environmental data. This allows patterns to be compared across different locations and depths, making the information more useful for analysis. The system can help researchers identify problem areas, compare changes between sampling points, and better understand how conditions vary within a body of water.  The data output is another major part of AquaScan’s connection to the theme. Scientific tools are not only useful because they collect information, but because they make that information easier to understand. AquaScan’s interface and digital output are designed to display collected measurements through maps, graphs, heat maps, and layered depth-based views. These formats help turn raw sensor readings into visual information that can support research, restoration planning, and environmental decision-making. This makes AquaScan more than a mobile sensing platform. It becomes a complete tool for collecting and communicating scientific data.  The connection to the theme is strongest in the way AquaScan improves the research process:  ●   Direct scientific measurement:   The probe collects water-quality data using multiple sensors instead of relying only on visual observation or surface assumptions.  ●   Depth-aware data collection:   Measurements can be taken below the surface, allowing researchers to study changes through the water column.  ●   Location-based sampling:   GPS-linked readings make it easier to compare conditions across different areas of a water body.  ●   Improved data interpretation: Graphs, maps, heat maps, and depth profiles help make collected data easier to understand and use.  ●   Environmental research support:   The system can help identify conditions related to oxygen stress, turbidity, runoff, chemical imbalance, and limited light penetration.  ●   Practical field use:   AquaScan is designed to make detailed water-quality monitoring more accessible for researchers, restoration groups, and environmental organizations.  Overall, AquaScan communicates the theme by showing how engineering can expand what scientists are able to observe and measure. By combining direct sensing, depth-based sampling, location tracking, and visual data output, AquaScan provides a practical tool for studying aquatic environments more clearly. The system supports scientific discovery by helping researchers gather stronger data, recognize environmental patterns, and make better-informed decisions about the health of water bodies.
