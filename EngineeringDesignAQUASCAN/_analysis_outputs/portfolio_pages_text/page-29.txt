Probe Deployment and Depth Stability  ●   Issue:   The probe had to lower into the water column without excessive drag, swinging, or floating upward. It also needed to stay relatively vertical so depth-based readings would be more consistent.  ●   Refinement made:   The probe body was designed with stabilizing fins to reduce unwanted movement through the water. Additional internal weight was used so the probe would sink instead of floating near the surface. The spool was also designed with a wider cable-wrapping surface to reduce tangling and limit how many times the tether had to wrap around itself.  ●   Result:   These refinements improved probe deployment, retrieval, and underwater stability. However, depth measurement remains a limitation because the current system estimates depth from tether deployment rather than measuring it directly at the probe. A future version would improve this by adding a pressure-based depth sensor.  6.   Light and UV Sensor Accuracy Issue  ●   Issue:   The light and UV sensors did not produce readings as accurately as expected because the clear printed probe material was not fully transparent. The printed part came out more frosted than clear, which diffused the light before it reached the sensors.  ●   Refinement planned:   A future version would replace the printed clear section with a true optical window made from a clearer material, such as acrylic or polycarbonate. Instead of printing the entire cap in clear filament, the probe could use a smaller sealed window directly over the light and UV sensors.  ●   Result:   This issue showed that material choice affects sensor accuracy, especially for sensors that depend on light transmission. The rest of the probe could remain printed and sealed, but light-based sensors need a clearer viewing path to produce more reliable readings.
