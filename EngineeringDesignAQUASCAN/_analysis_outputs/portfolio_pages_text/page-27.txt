By connecting each reading to a sampling location, AquaScan made it possible to compare different areas visually and identify where certain conditions were stronger or weaker. This supports the idea that mobile monitoring can provide more useful environmental data than relying only on fixed sensors or isolated manual samples.  The data output itself was another important result. AquaScan did not only collect raw sensor readings; it organized them into tables, graphs, and spatial visualizations. This made the results easier to interpret and communicate. The depth graphs helped show changes through the water column, while the heat maps helped show variation across the pond. This supported the project’s connection to “Engineering the Tools of Scientific Discovery” because AquaScan acted as a tool that collects, organizes, and visualizes scientific information instead of only producing disconnected measurements.  Overall, the testing results showed that AquaScan is a plausible and effective solution for collecting location-based and depth-aware water-quality data in calm, shallow, and sheltered environments. The prototype successfully demonstrated the main engineering concept: a mobile vessel can travel to sampling   points, deploy a sensor probe, collect readings below the surface, and produce organized data outputs for analysis. While future versions could improve autonomy, depth measurement accuracy, sensor calibration, and long-term field reliability, the testing completed for this prototype proved that the core system works and that the design has clear potential as a practical environmental monitoring tool.
