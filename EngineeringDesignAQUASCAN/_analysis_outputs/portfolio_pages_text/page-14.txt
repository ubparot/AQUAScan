Sensor Integration:  All major sensing components in AquaScan are housed in the probe so that measurements can be taken at selected depths rather than only at the surface. The probe contains an ESP32 inside the sealed body, and it receives power and transmits sensor data through the same marine cable used as the tether. Data is sent back to the vessel and communicated to the onboard Arduino through the RS-485 connection, allowing the probe to function as an integrated sensing unit without requiring its own battery.  Sensors were mounted through designed openings in the probe shell, with sealing handled using a combination of O-rings and silicone sealant depending on whether the component needed to remain removable or could be permanently fixed in place. This gave the system the ability to measure outside conditions directly while still protecting the internal electronics.  Scientific Sensor Suite:  The probe was designed as a multi-parameter water-quality sensing package, allowing AquaScan to collect several related measurements at the same location and depth. This matters because water quality is not defined by one value alone. A stronger scientific picture comes from comparing several conditions together and seeing how they change through the water column.  1.   Dissolved Oxygen Sensor  Dissolved oxygen is one of the most important measurements in the system because it directly affects whether aquatic organisms can survive in a given part of the water column. Oxygen levels can drop significantly at deeper depths, especially when the water becomes stratified, meaning it separates into layers that do not mix easily. When that happens, deeper water may receive less oxygen from the surface, creating low-oxygen bottom waters that place stress on fish and other aquatic life. By measuring dissolved oxygen at selected depths, AquaScan can detect these hidden subsurface conditions much more effectively than a surface-only system.  2.   Turbidity Sensor  Turbidity measures how cloudy the water is due to suspended particles such as sediment, organic matter, or other material in the water. This is scientifically useful because increased turbidity can reduce light penetration, affect aquatic plant growth, and indicate runoff, erosion, or other disturbances. Measuring turbidity at depth helps show whether suspended material is concentrated near the surface or persists lower in the water column as well.  3.   Total Dissolved Solids Sensor  Total dissolved solids indicate how much dissolved material is present in the water. This helps reveal changes in water composition that may be linked to runoff, nutrient input, or other environmental influences. While it is not usually interpreted by itself, it becomes much more useful when compared with the other probe measurements at the same depth.  4.   Temperature Sensor  Temperature is important because it helps reveal thermal layering in the water column. Even if temperature changes are not always dramatic, they still matter because temperature affects oxygen availability, biological activity, and the way water layers mix. In AquaScan, temperature helps provide the physical context needed to interpret the rest of the measurements more accurately.  5.   pH Sensor  pH measures how acidic or basic the water is.
