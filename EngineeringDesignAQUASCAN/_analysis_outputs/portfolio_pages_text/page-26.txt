Evaluation of Results  The pool and pond tests showed that AquaScan was able to function as more than a basic floating prototype. The tests demonstrated that the vessel could float under load, move through water, deploy the probe, collect sensor readings, and organize the results into usable digital outputs. This was important because the selected solution was designed around one main goal: collecting water-quality data that is linked to both location and depth. The testing process showed that AquaScan could complete that workflow in controlled conditions and in a real calm-water environment.  The pool test was useful because it allowed the team to evaluate AquaScan in a controlled setting before moving into a less predictable environment. During this stage, the team was able to check flotation, motor response, waterproofing, probe deployment, and live sensor output. The pool environment made it easier to observe whether the boat remained balanced, whether the probe could lower and retrieve correctly, and whether the system could collect readings without immediately risking the full prototype in natural water. This test confirmed that the major mechanical, electrical, and waterproofing systems were functional enough for field testing.  The pond test provided the strongest evidence that AquaScan’s concept was plausible because it tested the system in a more realistic monitoring environment. Unlike the pool, the pond had natural water conditions, outdoor lighting, turbidity variation, and different sampling locations. AquaScan collected data across multiple points, and the exported graphs, tables, and heat maps showed that the readings were not uniform across the test area. Values such as turbidity, dissolved oxygen, temperature, conductivity, total dissolved solids, light, and ultraviolet readings changed across sampling points and depths. This supported the main purpose of AquaScan: water quality cannot always be represented accurately by one surface reading or one fixed monitoring location.  The depth-based graphs were especially important because they showed how readings changed as the probe moved through the water column. Even in a relatively shallow pond environment, the sensor outputs showed variation with depth, which supports the need for depth-aware monitoring. These results showed that AquaScan can collect data in a way that helps reveal vertical changes that surface-only systems may miss. This is one of the most important advantages of the selected solution because the deployable probe allows the vessel to collect below-surface information without requiring a fixed buoy station or manual depth sampling.  The location-based results also supported the value of the system.
