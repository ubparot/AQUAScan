II. Possible Refinements  Upon completing AquaScan’s current prototype, these were the main refinements identified that would improve the vessel’s accuracy, autonomy, and overall usefulness as a water-quality monitoring platform if given more development time:  1.   Sonar and Bathymetry Mapping System  -   A major refinement would be adding a downward-facing sonar system to the bottom of AquaScan. The current prototype collects water-quality readings at different GPS locations and probe depths, but sonar would add information about the underwater environment itself. This would allow AquaScan to connect sensor readings to bottom depth, underwater slope, sediment areas, or other submerged features. With this upgrade, AquaScan could create basic bathymetry maps while also collecting water-quality data, making the system more useful for ponds, marinas, creeks, and protected waterways.  -   Testing means: The sonar system could be tested first in a pool or pond with known depths, then compared to manual depth measurements taken from the same points. After calibration, sonar data could be combined with GPS points and sensor readings to verify that AquaScan can map both water quality and bottom depth during the same mission.  2.   Direct Probe Depth Measurement  -   One of the most important limitations of the current prototype is that probe depth is estimated through the deployment system rather than measured directly at the probe. Tether length and spool rotation can provide a usable estimate, but depth accuracy can be affected by uneven cable wrapping, changing spool radius, tether angle, or probe movement in the water. A future version would use a pressure-based depth sensor inside the probe so each water-quality reading is connected to the probe’s actual depth instead of only the commanded deployment distance. This would make depth profiles more accurate and make AquaScan’s data stronger for comparing surface-level and deeper water conditions.  -   Testing means: The probe could be tested at known depths in a pool or marked water column, with each depth sensor reading compared to a measured reference depth. The team could then calculate the error at different depths and adjust the software calibration until the probe produces consistent depth values. This would show whether the new sensor improves accuracy compared to estimating depth from the winch system alone.  3.   Improved Probe Buoyancy and Depth-Control System  -   Another limitation of the current prototype is that the probe relies mainly on weight and tether control to move through the water column. This works for basic depth testing, but it can make the probe more dependent on the winch system and tether tension. A more advanced probe could use an adjustable ballast or intake-based buoyancy system to descend, hold depth, and rise more smoothly. This would reduce strain on the tether, improve probe stability, and make it easier to collect readings at specific depths, especially when the water is less controlled than a pool.  -   Testing means: The improved probe could be tested by measuring descent rate, stability, and ability to hold depth in still water and slightly moving water. The team could compare the current weighted probe to the buoyancy-controlled version to see whether the refinement reduces swinging, improves depth stability, and produces more consistent readings at each depth level.  4.
