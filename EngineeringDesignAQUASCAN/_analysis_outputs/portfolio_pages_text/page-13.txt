12  Probe and Tether Design:  The probe was designed to sink rather than float, keeping the tether in tension to reduce slack and improve depth consistency. Its shape was refined to reduce drag during lowering and retrieval. The probe connects to the vessel through a marine cable that serves as both the deployment tether and the power/signal link. The cable wraps cleanly onto the spool without tangling, eliminating the need for a separate detangling mechanism. A slip ring maintains continuous electrical connection during spool rotation, preventing cable twisting and reducing torque transfer into the system.  Depth Positioning:  Probe depth is estimated from calibrated spool rotation. As the deployment motor rotates the spool, the length of cable released can be approximated from spool rotation, allowing the probe to be positioned at selected depths. The spool was designed long enough that the cable wraps without significantly increasing the radius, keeping the arc length released per rotation nearly constant.  ●   Rotation-based depth estimate:   spool rotation is used to approximate tether length deployed  ●   Long spool geometry:   keeps spool radius nearly constant during deployment  ●   Slip ring connection:   maintains wire continuity while allowing rotation  ●   Tensioned tether:   the sinking probe helps keep the line straight and reduces slack  ●   Stationary depth sampling:   the vessel is intended to stop at each monitoring point before deploying the probe, improving depth control and measurement consistency  ●   Worm gear retention:   provides a self-locking effect to help prevent the probe from freely dropping if power is lost  ●   Balanced component placement:   positions the winch/probe assembly opposite the battery to improve weight distribution across the platform  Probe Design and Sensor Integration  The probe is the scientific core of AquaScan and the part of the system most directly responsible for solving the monitoring problem defined in this project. While the surface vessel provides mobility, positioning, and deployment, the probe is the part that actually collects the depth-based environmental data. This makes the probe essential to AquaScan’s value as a monitoring system because it turns the vessel from a mobile platform into an active scientific measurement tool.  Probe Design:  The probe body is approximately 16 in long and 3 in in diameter and was manufactured from PETG as a multi-part 3D printed assembly. Its shape was designed to reduce drag during deployment, transit, and retrieval, with fins that help keep the probe more stable and aligned as it moves through the water. The probe was also designed to sink rather than float, which keeps the tether in tension and improves consistency in depth positioning.
