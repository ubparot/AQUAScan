Executive Assessment

AquaScan has a real national-level advantage: you built a functioning vessel, probe, Unity interface, and data workflow instead of only proposing a theoretical system. The current weakness is not effort or ambition. The weakness is that the evidence and display do not yet make the project feel inevitable to a judge.

The national version should move from “we built a strong prototype” to “we defined measurable requirements, tested against them, found failures, improved the design, and proved the solution is the best fit for this specific monitoring gap.”

Most Valuable Strength

You have physical execution: custom boat, probe, software, and field data. That is harder to fake than a polished theoretical concept.

Most Dangerous Weakness

The project still reads too narratively. Nationals judges need fast evidence: tables, measured results, calibration, comparisons, and clear pass/fail validation.

Best Strategic Move

Make validation the centerpiece. Show measured performance and uncertainty before adding more features.

What Not To Do

Do not add more paragraphs, more feature slides, or more speculative future upgrades until the core proof is stronger.

Critical Critiques

1. Compliance Is A Gate, Not A Detail

Verify the display dimensions immediately. The current guide limits the display to 15 inches deep, 2.5 feet wide, and 4 feet high. If the physical trifold is wider than 2.5 feet, this could become a rules violation. The current guide also requires a clearly labeled device or pocket on the front of the display to hold the USB flash drive.

  • Measure the display with everything set up exactly as judged.
  • Label the USB pocket clearly and make it look intentional.
  • Provide clear one-button or one-switch instructions if judges activate electronics.
  • Confirm all power is dry-cell/photo-voltaic and fits within the display area.

2. The Display Is Too Text-Heavy

The board currently looks professional, but it reads like printed portfolio pages mounted on a trifold. Nationals judges will not read paragraph-heavy boards deeply during first-pass judging. They need a strong visual argument from several feet away.

  • Center panel: large AquaScan identity, one-sentence thesis, hero prototype image, and “how it works” diagram.
  • Left panel: problem, monitoring gap, alternatives matrix, and design requirements.
  • Right panel: measured validation results, field data visuals, failures/refinements, and impact.
  • Use fewer panels, larger figures, larger captions, and less explanatory prose.

3. Real Data Is A Strength, But It Needs Controls

Your pond testing is more credible than purely theoretical submissions, but it is not yet judge-proof. Right now the argument is mostly that readings varied across points and depths. Nationals-level evidence should show accuracy, repeatability, and uncertainty.

  • Calibrate each sensor and show calibration curves or before/after calibration tables.
  • Compare readings with a handheld meter, chemical test kit, borrowed probe, or known standard where possible.
  • Repeat the same point/depth multiple times and report variation.
  • Show known-depth testing: commanded depth vs actual measured depth.
  • Report GPS waypoint accuracy: target coordinate vs actual stopped coordinate.

4. The Portfolio Needs More Engineering Artifacts

The selected solution section should look more like an engineering design packet. Photos and CAD renders are useful, but the highest-scoring version should include visual representations that follow engineering conventions.

  • System block diagram: operator interface, communication, Arduino/ESP32, sensors, motors, battery, winch.
  • Electrical schematic or wiring diagram with power rails, fuses, controllers, ESCs, sensors, and tether.
  • Mechanical drawing with hull dimensions, draft, payload placement, and deployment geometry.
  • Control flowchart for manual control, waypoint navigation, sampling, deployment, and data logging.
  • Data pipeline diagram from sensor reading to CSV to graphs/maps/heat maps.

Evidence Upgrade Plan

The biggest improvement before nationals is a disciplined validation campaign. Keep it realistic: you do not need a perfect commercial instrument. You need to show that you know what the prototype can and cannot prove.

Test What To Measure Why Judges Care
Depth Accuracy Commanded/deployed depth vs actual depth at known marks. Your main claim is depth-aware monitoring. Depth error must be quantified.
Sensor Calibration Known standards or reference readings for temperature, pH, turbidity, TDS/conductivity, and dissolved oxygen. Variation in readings only matters if the readings are credible.
Repeatability Three to five repeated readings at the same location and depth. Shows whether the system produces consistent data instead of one-off values.
Waypoint Accuracy Target GPS point vs actual stop location; distance error. Supports the claim that readings are location-linked and repeatable.
Deployment Reliability Number of successful lower/hold/retrieve cycles out of total attempts. Turns a live demo into measured engineering reliability.
Runtime and Power Battery voltage drop, current draw, mission duration, and estimated operational time. Shows practical feasibility and helps answer scale/use questions.
Leak Testing Duration submerged, inspected components, pass/fail, and any water intrusion. Waterproofing is a core risk for the entire design.

Objective Method Measured Result Issue Found Refinement Retest Result

Idea and Positioning Critique

The idea is strongest when positioned as a low-cost, shallow-water, depth-aware monitoring platform. Do not let judges compare you directly against ocean-grade autonomous survey vessels. Define the exact gap you solve.

Recommended one-sentence positioning:

AquaScan is a low-cost, semi-autonomous shallow-water research vessel that collects location-tagged and depth-tagged water-quality data using a deployable multi-sensor probe, filling the gap between fixed buoys, manual sampling, drones, and large commercial survey systems.

Questions The Portfolio Should Answer Fast

  • Who is the user: researchers, restoration groups, marinas, schools, local agencies, or environmental organizations?
  • What exact environment is it for: ponds, marinas, sheltered creeks, shallow lakes, protected nearshore water?
  • What does it replace or improve: manual sampling, fixed stations, surface-only RC/USV sampling, or drone imagery?
  • What is the approximate cost compared with existing commercial monitoring systems?
  • What is the current prototype capable of, and what is future work?

Be Precise About Autonomy

Use “semi-autonomous” carefully. If pond testing was primarily manual with waypoint features tested, say that directly. Overclaiming autonomy gives judges an easy opening. A stronger statement is: manual control has been field-tested, waypoint navigation has been prototyped, and full autonomous route execution is a planned refinement.

Competitive Positioning

The concern that “water sensing” may feel less exciting than a microscope, a space-cleaning concept, or an unusual agriculture invention is understandable. The fix is not to change the project. The fix is to stop presenting AquaScan as a water-quality boat and start presenting it as a scientific discovery instrument.

Weak framing

We made a boat that collects water-quality data.

Strong framing

AquaScan reveals hidden aquatic conditions by collecting sensor readings linked to both GPS location and depth.

Why This Fits The Theme

The annual theme rewards tools that expand what scientists can observe, measure, and interpret. A microscope is compelling because it reveals something invisible. AquaScan should be positioned the same way: surface water can look normal while important conditions exist below. AquaScan turns those hidden conditions into a depth-aware, location-based scientific dataset.

Recommended core sentence:

AquaScan turns water from a surface-level observation into a depth-aware, location-based scientific dataset.

Your Competitive Advantage

Many theoretical projects may sound bigger or more futuristic. AquaScan’s advantage is that it is an integrated physical tool that was actually built and tested. The strongest national-level stance is not “ours is less theoretical.” It is:

  • Other teams proposed tools. AquaScan is a working scientific instrument.
  • Other teams may describe possible impact. AquaScan can show a measured workflow from field collection to visualized data.
  • Other teams may present a concept. AquaScan can demonstrate vessel movement, probe deployment, data capture, and interpretation.

Make The Findings More Dramatic

The pond findings are useful, but they can feel underwhelming because natural variation may look like random graph movement. Add one controlled demonstration before nationals where the surface appears normal but the lower water column is intentionally different. Then the scientific result becomes: surface-only monitoring missed the condition, but AquaScan’s depth profile detected it.

Controlled Demo What It Proves Why It Helps The Story
Warm top layer, colder bottom layer Temperature changes with depth. Shows AquaScan can detect vertical stratification instead of only surface conditions.
Freshwater over denser saltwater layer TDS/conductivity changes below the surface. Creates a clear hidden layer that a surface reading would miss.
Cloudy/turbid bottom layer Turbidity can vary by depth. Connects directly to runoff, sediment, and water clarity concerns.
Depth markers in a pool or test tank Commanded probe depth can be compared to actual depth. Strengthens the credibility of all depth-based data.

Display/Presentation Reframe

Add a major visual section titled “What Surface Data Misses.” It should compare surface sampling, fixed buoys, drones, and AquaScan in one fast-read graphic.

Method What It Sees What It Misses
Manual surface sample One point at one depth. Vertical changes and repeated spatial patterns.
Fixed buoy Continuous data at one location. Fine-scale spatial variation across a water body.
Drone imaging Large-area surface patterns. Subsurface chemistry and depth profiles.
AquaScan Location-tagged and depth-tagged sensor data. More complete profiles in shallow, sheltered, and nearshore environments.

The final story should be simple: AquaScan is not cool because it is a boat. It is cool because it lets scientists discover hidden water conditions that surface observation can miss.

Presentation Critique

The current slideshow is strong visually but too much of it is a feature tour. For nationals, the presentation should be built around proof. Judges should hear the main claim, see the prototype, then see measured evidence.

Time Focus Goal

Judge Q&A Prep

  • Why is AquaScan better than a fixed buoy network?
  • Why is AquaScan better than drone-based water monitoring?
  • How accurate are the sensors?
  • How do you know probe depth?
  • How many samples did you collect, and how repeatable were they?
  • What failed during testing, and what did you change?
  • What is the projected cost of the prototype and a refined version?
  • What would you improve first with another month?
  • What parts were student-built, and what parts were purchased components?
  • What safety risks did you control?

Portfolio Rewrite Priorities

Do not make the portfolio longer just to look more complete. Make the judge’s scoring job easier. Each section should map visibly to a rubric criterion.

Problem Definition

Start with one concise problem sentence. Then use bullets or a mini-table to define the monitoring gaps: depth, spatial coverage, temporal resolution, interpretation.

Information Gathering

Add specific evidence about current systems and their limitations. Include cost, deployment constraints, or data limitations where possible.

Possible Solutions

Use a weighted decision matrix instead of only prose. Score buoy, drone, standard USV, and AquaScan concept against weighted criteria.

Selected Solution

Lead with diagrams and engineering drawings. Put paragraphs after visuals, not before them.

Iteration Process

Use test/refinement/retest tables. Show failures clearly. Judges reward mature engineering, not pretending everything worked.

Communication of Solution

Make this one page extremely clear: how AquaScan meets the theme, what scientific discovery it enables, and why the data output matters.

Bottom Line

AquaScan can be competitive at nationals because it has something many entries will not: a real integrated prototype with field testing. The remaining work is to make the proof undeniable. Prioritize validation, display clarity, and engineering documentation over additional features.

The national-level version should make one argument repeatedly: AquaScan solves a specific water-quality monitoring gap by combining mobility, depth-aware sensing, location tagging, and visual data output, and the team proved the core workflow through measured testing and iteration.
